Justice in Judgment

Unveiling (Hidden) Bias in LLM-assisted Peer Reviews

Sai Suresh Macharla Vasu1,  Ivaxi Sheth2,  Hui-Po Wang2,  Ruta Binkyte2,  Mario Fritz2

1Saarland University  ·  2CISPA Helmholtz Center for Information Security

Paper arXiv Code

Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to assist in academic peer review, raising important questions about whether they replicate or amplify human biases. In this work, we study four axes of potential bias in LLM-assisted reviewing: institutional affiliation, author gender, academic seniority, and publication history. Using ICLR 2025 submissions as a testbed, we design a counterfactual evaluation framework: the same paper is reviewed by 9 LLMs under different author profiles, keeping all other factors fixed. Our findings reveal that most models systematically assign higher scores to papers attributed to prestigious affiliations, senior researchers, and prolific authors — biases that mirror well-documented patterns in human review. Gender bias is more nuanced, with models showing inconsistent directional effects. These results highlight the risk of deploying LLMs in high-stakes scholarly decisions and call for bias-aware evaluation protocols.

Experimental Setup

Dataset

252 papers from ICLR 2025 (accepted & rejected). Each paper reviewed under multiple author profiles per bias dimension.

9 LLMs Evaluated

GPT-4o-mini Gemini Flash Lite LLaMA 3.1-70B LLaMA 3.1-8B Mistral-Small-22B Mistral-8B DeepSeek-Qwen-32B DeepSeek-R1-8B QwQ

4 Bias Dimensions

  • Affiliation — prestigious vs. less-ranked institutions
  • Gender — male vs. female author names
  • Seniority — Senior PI vs. undergraduate
  • Publication History — 100 vs. 0 top-tier papers

Evaluation Protocol

Counterfactual design: identical paper content reviewed under different author metadata. LLM score compared directly between conditions for each paper.
